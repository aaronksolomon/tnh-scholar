Successive Causation and the Continuity of Life

2. The question of successive causation is also the question of continuity in existence. Although all phenomena are impermanent and ever changing, nothing is utterly annihilated. There is no individual entity that is eternal and unchanging, yet there are always continuous, unbroken “streams” of phenomena. All things transform without cease according to definite laws. The Buddha gave particular attention to explaining the continuity of the life-stream, that is, the phenomena of sentient beings. He taught that the fundamental driving forces of birth-and-death and transmigration are ignorance and craving. Sentient beings themselves are the composites of the whole of their past experiences and karmic causes. Through present karmic causes, sentient beings move toward future stages of life. From the logical point of view, he clarified the law of heterogeneous causes and heterogeneous results. From the psychological point of view, he explained the law of homogeneous causes and equal-flow results. From the metaphysical point of view, he presented the law of the Twelvefold Dependent Origination. Even though this was twenty-five centuries ago, when terminology and modes of expression were still limited, the texts of the Four Āgamas were already able to express these subtle teachings.

In sum, the Buddhist view of causation, in its narrower sense, is simply the Law of Causality; but in its broader sense, it is not confined to causal relations of a purely theoretical kind. The Buddhist understanding of causation also embraces ethical and liberative relations; its breadth extends throughout the ten directions, and its length penetrates past, future, and present.
