The Conditioned Worldview and the Principle of Dependent Arising

The worldview expounded by the Buddha is a worldview of causes and conditions (Paticca samuppada). All phenomena depend upon one another for their establishment. All are intimately related to one another. Without that relation, no thing could be established. That relation is precisely causes and conditions. In the scriptures, the terms NHÂN (hetu), DUYÊN (paccaya), CONDITION (nidana), and ARISING (samudaya) are all different names indicating that relation. The Buddha defined causes and conditions as follows: “If this is, that is; if this arises, that arises; if this is not, that is not; if this ceases, that ceases.”

(* If this exists, that exists; if this arises, that arises; if this is absent, that is absent; if this ceases, that ceases....)

To say, “If this is, that is; if this is not, that is not,” is to present the relation among things existing simultaneously. To say, “If this arises, that arises; if this ceases, that ceases,” is to present the relation among things existing at different times. All dharmas arise, cease, and exist within this very intimate web of mutual relation; no dharma (phenomenon) can exist independently and absolutely. When the cause comes first and the effect later, this is called temporally successive causation; when cause and effect occur at the same time, this is called simultaneous causation. One cause may have different effects according to the accompanying secondary conditions. Therefore, there can be no absolute cause, nor can there be any absolute effect.

The world is woven out of systems of simultaneous causes and conditions (from the standpoint of space) and systems of temporally successive causes and conditions (from the standpoint of time). This is the Buddhist conception of causality. In the sutras and treatises, the term conditioned dharmas (Samkhata dhamma) refers precisely to the phenomena of this conditioned world. All are impermanent and changing, precisely because of causal and conditional relatedness; for within a conditioned world, there can be no eternal and everlasting phenomena. It is on the basis of this conception of dependent arising that Buddhism rejects the idea of the world’s creation by a self-possessed Supreme God. This is something we cannot fail to acknowledge.

According to the Huayan school, the whole universe is an infinitely interpenetrating system of dependent arising. Huayan doctrine, thus, was established upon the conception of causal relations already found in the Agamas. The Abhidharma works of Southern Buddhism also classified causes and conditions for purposes of observation and understanding, and this was indeed a principal task of the Theravada scholastics. (The Mahapakarana distinguishes as many as 24 conditions; fascicle 25 of the Sariputra Abhidharma-sastra distinguishes 10 conditions; the Sarvastivadins maintain 4 conditions.)

Here, we shall only briefly examine two kinds: temporally successive causation and simultaneous causation.
